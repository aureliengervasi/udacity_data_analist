Titanic Data set¶

Contains demographics and passenger information from 891 of the 2224 passengers and crew on board the Titanic. You can view a description of this dataset on the Kaggle website, where the data was obtained.

https://www.kaggle.com/c/titanic/data

Following observations can be made:

  • We have 12 kind of variables for each 891 passenger from the Titanic.
  • Some variables are not available for all passengers
  • Data types are correct regarding to the kind of information they describe.
  • 2 categories of passengers: those who survived (Survived = 1) and those who did not (Survived = 0)
  • 3 categories of passenger class
  • Age ranging from 0.42 (less than 1 year old?) to 80 years old
  • Siblings and spouse count ranging from 0 to 8 (large family...)
  • Parent and children count ranging from 0 to 6
  • Fare ranging from 0 (crew?) to 512.3
  • There are only 204 entries for the Cabin variable
  • Passengers embarked from 3 different ports

Question¶

Following our first observations about the data sets, the main question we will study in the following paragraphs is:

  • What factors made people more likely to survive?

In that case, the Survived column would be our dependent variable, whereas the 11 others would be our independent variables.

Function to plot automatically the survival rate per category for one variable¶

In [114]:
df2 = 0 
df2 = df.copy() # Copy df dataframe in order to keep an 'untouched' Dataframe

def barplot_survival_rate(col, legend, xlabels = None):   
    if xlabels== None:
        xlabels = df2.groupby(col)['Survived'].mean().keys()
    values = df2.groupby(col)['Survived'].mean()
    N = len(values)
    ind = np.arange(N)    
    width = 0.4       

    plt.bar(ind, values, width, label=legend)
    plt.axhline(df2.Survived.mean(), color = 'r', ls = '--', label='mean survival rate')
    plt.legend()

    plt.ylabel('Survival rate')
    plt.title(col)
    plt.xticks(ind + width/2., xlabels)
    plt.yticks(np.arange(0, 1.1, 0.1))
    plt.show()

Conclusions¶

Gender¶

With 74% of the women having survived the disaster, compared to only 19% for men, gender show a great correlation with survival rate. The causation cannot be derived here, but a reasonable guess could be that during that disaster, women were put first in the lifeboats.

Passenger class¶

With 63% of the first class passengers having survived the disaster (compared to 47% for second class passengers, and 24% for third class passengers), passenger class is also a variable showing a strong correlation with survival rate: the higher the class, the higher the chances to survive.

A logical explanation for this correlation could be that passenger from the upper class tended to be on the ship upper levels, which puts them closer to the lifeboats.

Age¶

The first bin (age < 10 years) shows a significantly higher survival rate (61%). It can be guessed that children and babies were more inclined to be helped and protected during the catastrophe.

Except for the first bin (age < 10 years), the survival rate stays relatively constant between the different age categories. I have discarded the last two categories from my analysis, as they only concern 7 passengers out of 891.

Siblings and spouse count / Parent and children count¶

The trend is less obvious here. It seems that lone passengers where less probable to survive (30% survival rate) than passenger coming with family members. This appears at least if we are talking about 2 to 4 people groups.

Groups with higher family member counts seem to be associated with lower survival rates (under 34%). But the data for these big groups is scarce, which makes it difficult to justify the correlation.

Fare¶

Ticket price is logically associated with class level. Hence, as for the Passenger class variable, we can see a clear correlation between Fare and survival rate. The higher the price, the higher the survival rate.

Cabin¶

There are a lot of missing entries for this variable. These entries are strings which represent cabin number. It could be interesting to see the dependency between the location of the cabin and the survival rate. But without the titanic map, it is difficult to assess the cabin locations.

Hence, I only focused here on the relationship between cabin booking, and passenger class. As it could have been guessed, the cabin were mainly booked by first class passenger (86%), which explains the very high correlation between 'having booked a cabin' and 'having survived to the disaster' (67% survival rate).

It can be guessed that people having booked a cabin where on the upper levels of the boat, which must have helped to jump into a lifeboat.

Port¶

According to the data, people coming from Cherbourg showed a higher survival rate than the others (55%), which was a bit unexpected.

But after digging a bit the question, it appears that the passengers from Cherbourg were essentially from class 1 and 2, which, as we saw before, was correlated with a high survival rate. This helps to explain this unexpected result concerning the Embarked variable.

I also tried to figure out whether there were more women coming from Cherbourg. We can see indeed a women ratio slightly higher than for people coming from Southampton, which goes in the direction of the correlation that we saw before.

Limitations¶

Only 891 from the 2344 Titanic passengers have been studied here. Chances are that they have been randomly selected and represent correctly the overall population, but, as with every sampling process, there are differences between the sample and the population characteristics.

All variables were not equally filled with Data. In particular, Age was missing 177 entries out of 891 passengers. I chose to replace the missing values by the variable mean, but as we saw, it has an impact on the sample standard deviation. Caution should be taken if a statistical test is done on this data.

Also, all the previous remarks on the possible correlation between the different variables were based on statistical calculation, but not on statistical testing. To validate these conclusion, additional testing should be pursued.

Summary¶

Through this analysis, we were able to observe different correlation between survival rate and the other variable. In particular, being a woman seemed to be correlated with a higher survival rate (74%) than when being a man (19%). Age seemed also to be an important factor, but only for kids (age < 10 years) who showed a survival rate higher than the mean (61% against 38%). Last but not least, passenger from higher class (1 and 2) showed also higher survival rate (resp. 63% and 47%).

But it is important to underline that these observations were not validated by statistical testing, which could be an opportunity to extend the analysis further on.
